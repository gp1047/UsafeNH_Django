Helpful Answers

The term sexual assault refers to any sexual contact that occurs without explicit consent. Sexual assault can take many forms, but it is never the victim’s fault. Forms of sexual assault include: penetration of the victim’s body (also known as rape), attempted rape, forcing a victim to perform sexual acts, and fondling or unwanted sexual touching.

Definition:
Consent is two people (or more) deciding together to do the same thing, at the same time, in the same way, with each other, without coercion, intimidation, manipulation, physical force, power disparity, mental illness, incapacitation, intoxication, and/or threats.

Active consent:
Consent is active, not passive. There must always be active consent on both sides. A person who consents has the right to change their mind at any point in time. If they do, the sexual activity must stop immediately.

Legal age of consent:
In New Hampshire, the legal age of consent is 16.

Yes. You always have the right to decide what sexual contact you consent to, with whom, and when you are comfortable with it. Active consent on a prior occasion does not provide consent for future contact. Someone who the victim knows perpetrates approximately 2/3 of all sexual assaults.

[SOURCE: U.S. Department of Justice, National Crime Victimization Study: 2009-2013]

Go to a safe place:
It is important that you are in a place where you feel physically and mentally safe in order to decide how to take care of yourself.

Seek medical care:
As soon as possible, go to a hospital emergency department. Even if you think that you do not have any physical injuries, it is important to have a medical examination. Having a medical exam is also a way for you to preserve physical evidence of a sexual assault if you decide to report the crime to law enforcement. In addition to the exam, medical providers can offer you emergency contraception to prevent pregnancy, medication to help prevent HIV and counseling to help determine your risk for HIV, and medication to prevent many other sexually transmitted infections.

Do not shower and try to save all evidence
In an effort to preserve all physical evidence of the assault:
• If possible, do not shower, bathe, douche, eat, drink, urinate, defecate, wash your hands, or brush your teeth until after you have had a medical examination. If you have already done something on this list, it is still possible to have DNA evidence collected. Try to get to the hospital as soon as possible.
• Save all of the clothing you were wearing at the time of the assault.
• Do not clean or disturb anything in the area where the assault occurred.

If you think you have been drugged
If you suspect that you may have been given a “rape drug,” tell hospital personnel as soon as possible. Some drugs leave a person’s body system very quickly while others take longer. The best chance of detecting a drug will be if your first urination is what they get a sample of at the hospital.

You can choose to report the crime to campus authorities, campus police and/ or local police:
If you want to report the crime, notify the police immediately. Reporting the crime can help you regain a sense of personal power and control. You may also decide to report the crime to your school’s Title IX coordinator or other campus authority.

Call someone you trust:
• Call a friend, a family member, or someone else you trust who can be with you and provide support.
• You can also contact an advocate for free and confidential support.

Many choices and decisions
After a sexual assault, you have a lot of choices and decisions to make - e.g., about getting medical care, making a police report, and telling other people. You may have concerns about the impact of the assault and the reactions of friends and family members. You can get information and support by calling your local crisis center [SOURCE: Adapted from the Rape, Abuse & Incest National Network [RAINN] Website for N.H. College Students]

The decision to report to law enforcement:
The decision to report to law enforcement is yours alone to make if you are 18 or older

Reporting to law enforcement:

Initial meeting:
An officer will meet with you in a safe place to take a brief, initial report. They will also encourage you to seek medical attention to ensure that you receive proper care and follow up and offer crisis center services.

Follow-up meeting:
Arrangements will be made with you to meet in the near future with the investigating officer or detective assigned to follow up on the initial report. NH best practice allows for a crisis center advocate to be present if the victim desires, during the interview process.

Investigation:
Investigative follow up will continue with the collection of evidence, contact with potential witnesses and the suspect, as well as complete and thorough documentation.

If you want to remain anonymous:
A person who is over the age of 18 and has been sexually assaulted in NH can contact law enforcement to ask questions about the reporting process without identifying themselves in an effort to determine if they want to come forward and make a formal report. They may also provide some information about what happened to them; however, law enforcement cannot proceed with an investigation without an identified victim.

Reporting to Campus officials:
You may also decide to report to your campus. Your school’s Title IX Coordinator is responsible for making sure the school responds to all reports of sexual assault. Read more about how schools may respond here. They can also provide you with support and resources.

• In New Hampshire, the statute of limitations for Aggravated Felonious Sexual Assault and Felonious Sexual Assault is 6 years. If the suspect leaves the state during the 6 years, the statute of limitations may be extended.
• The statute of limitations for Sexual Assault is 1 year.
• If the victim is under the age of 18 at the time of the assault, the statute of limitations is 22 years after their 18th birthday (until age 40).

Before going to the hospital

Avoid bathing
If possible, avoid showering, bathing, using feminine hygiene products, urinating, defecating, washing your hands, or brushing your teeth before going to the hospital. This helps preserve evidence. If you have already done something on this list, it is still possible to have DNA evidence collected. Try to get to the hospital as soon as possible.

Bring a change of clothes
Bring a change of clothing with you if you are able. Any clothing worn at the time of the assault may be collected as evidence.

Bring someone you trust
You may wish to bring a trusted friend or family member with you for support.

Crisis center advocate
A local crisis center advocate will be available to provide free and confidential emotional support, information, and resources throughout the process.

Forensic exam procedures
This medical exam will determine if there are any physical injuries incurred during the assault, offer medication to help prevent sexually transmitted infections, and will collect data if you choose to report to law enforcement and proceed to trial

• The medical exam will determine if there are any physical injuries incurred during the assault, even if you do not think you have been injured. They will also offer emergency contraception to prevent pregnancy, medication to help prevent HIV and counseling to determine your risk for HIV, and medication to prevent many other sexually transmitted infections. During the exam you may also choose to have evidence collected in case you want to report to law enforcement. This can be done anonymously so that you have additional time to decide if you want to report. [SOURCE: http://www.nhcadsv.org/hospital_exam.cfm]


How much will it cost?

No charge for the exam
You will not be charged for the sexual assault examination. In addition, you will receive a voucher for a follow-up exam.

Ongoing health costs
If you decide to report to law enforcement, you may also be reimbursed for follow-up care and ongoing crime-related expenses (e.g., mental health treatment) after you leave the hospital.

Will the hospital call the police?
• No, not in NH and not if you do not want them to.
• The exceptions are for people under the age of 18 or who have suffered gunshot wound or other serious bodily injury.
• If you go to the hospital, you will receive medical care and still be able to decide whether or not you wish to report to law enforcement if you are over the age of 18.
• You also have the option to have forensic evidence collected anonymously. You will be given a serial number for the exam kit, which will be stored at the state lab for 60 days. If, during this time, you choose to report to law enforcement, the kit will be unsealed and tested for evidence.
Note: Anonymous evidence collection cannot be provided for those under the age of 18.
[SOURCE: http://doj.nh.gov/criminal/victim-assistance/documents/acute-care-protocol.pdf]

Is it too late?
DNA evidence is most reliably collected within 72 hours; however, evidence collection kits can be collected up to five days after an assault. However, the window of time for medication to prevent HIV and testing for detecting most drugs used to facilitate sexual assault is even shorter. It is best to get to the hospital as soon as possible. [SOURCE: http://www.nhcadsv.org/hospital_exam.cfm]

Campus sexual assault policy

On-campus

Counseling center
At some colleges, a counselor or counseling center can provide confidential support. To find out what options are available on your campus, and if they are confidential, please visit our resource section.

Support groups
You can check with your campus counseling center to see if any support groups are available at your school.

Title IX Coordinator
You may wish to speak with the Title IX Coordinator at your college. The Title IX Coordinator can be a very helpful resource; however, they will be required to take immediate action to address the assault

Role of the Title IX Coordinator
The Title IX Coordinator must take action to address the violence, remedy the harm and prevent the recurrence. The Title IX Coordinator can provide you with options for enhancing your safety, as well as for proceeding with a case. You may request confidentiality, but the Title IX Coordinator must consider safety concerns for the campus as a whole. School officials are not allowed to discourage you from continuing your education.

Off-campus

Local Crisis Center
Advocates from your local crisis center can be reached anytime of the day or night. They provide free and confidential support, advocacy, and information, no matter how long ago the assault happened – you don’t have to be in crisis to call a crisis center.

Schools must take immediate action to address all reports of sexual violence. This often includes an investigation and some kind of adjudication or conduct hearing. Schools have different policies and procedures established for how they proceed with a case. See your school’s policy here.
In addition to a hearing or investigation, the school must make sure that you can pursue your education safely and free of ongoing harassment, discrimination, or violence. There are a number of remedies or safety measures the school can implement. During the investigation and adjudication process, before the final decision is made, they can implement what are often called “interim relief measures.” These can include changes in housing, class or sports schedule, work-study or campus job assignment, or extracurricular activities and clubs. These accommodations should not pose an undue burden to you, including a financial burden. However, which measures are available to you will depend on the circumstances of your case, your school’s policy, and the choices you make regarding pursuing a formal complaint process. They can also implement an administrative No Contact order, barring the person who assaulted you from having contact with you.
Once the final outcome of your case is determined, these measures may or may not continue depending on the finding and any sanction that might be imposed.
At the beginning of this process, your school should present you with multiple options, including the option to file a formal complaint or providing you with other forms of assistance. You may request confidentiality, but your school will also have to consider its responsibility to ensure that your campus community is safe and free of gender-based discrimination.
They should also inform you of off-campus options and resources, including getting medical attention, reporting to police, and contacting the local crisis center. There are many differences between an on-campus response and an off-campus criminal justice system response. It is your choice to pursue one, both, or neither. A crisis advocate can help you understand these options.

Part of your school’s responsibility is to provide education around sexual assault and your school’s policies and procedures. The Title IX Coordinator is often involved in implementing this programing and training, so you can contact them for more information. During a traumatic event, a person may respond in ways that do not seem to make sense. They may react differently from how you imagine you might react or differently from how they have reacted in other situations. After the traumatic event, they may behave in ways that seem strange or talk about the situation in ways that do not add up. However, when you learn about trauma, these responses and behaviors can start to make a lot more sense.

There are a number of ways people may react to sexual assault — not a single “right” or “wrong” response. Sometimes, it’s easy to understand why a person is unable to fight back or run away; they could have been too intoxicated, drugged, or otherwise physically unable to do so. Sometimes a person makes a decision that fighting back is more dangerous, and they want to survive the assault with as little physical injury as possible; so, they comply or submit to the assailant, but that’s not the same thing as consent.
When met with a situation that threatens their survival or physical or emotional well-being, they often react with an automatic response designed to help. You might have heard of “fight or flight,” but it “fight, flight, freeze” more accurately captures the range of responses that a victim may have. In these situations, the brain triggers a release of hormones that affect our behavior. These can include the hormones that people often think of as stress hormones — the adrenaline that can make us feel “pumped up” and the cortisol that gives us energy. A surge of these hormones could result in fighting back or running away, if the victim is physically able to do so. There are also hormones that block pain, both physical and emotional, which can result in people feeling “out of it” or disconnected to the assault. Finally, there is also a hormone that causes positive feelings, which is another way to try to lessen the pain of the assault.
When someone experiences traumatic, parts of the brain that are responsible for survival are activated. The part of the brain that you use to make logical or rational decisions is often compromised. The way someone responds — flight, fight, or freeze — is a hardwired, automatic response meant to help us survive a critical threat. It isn’t a matter of making a right choice or wrong choice, because it isn’t even a conscious decision.

There is no wrong way to respond after the assault. Some people cry or yell, while others speak calmly. People can appear anxious, confused, withdrawn, or disconnected. They may have no affect at all or even laugh. There is no one correct way that all victims should respond.
As they try to talk about the assault, instead of going chronologically through the event, they may “jump around” in time or remember new details. This is also an outcome of the trauma suffered by the victim. Just as the hormone release associated with trauma can trigger an automatic behavioral response, it also interferes with memory. It does not mean the memories are false, just that the brain can’t store and retrieve them normally

It can take a long time to heal from a traumatic event. The ways they react might not make sense. Some common responses to trauma include:
• feelings of guilt, shame, embarrassment, anger at self, self-blame
• acting like nothing happened – wanting to make everything go back to normal
• academic struggles and/or difficulty concentrating
• hypervigilance (extreme concern with their safety), jumpiness and/or a heightened startle response
• not wanting to be touched
• changes in eating, sleeping, or how they dress
• becoming socially withdrawn
• difficulty trusting people
• increased drinking or drug use
• engaging in self-injury or risky behaviors

These behaviors are common — so, if you see your friend doing any of the above, try to be compassionate and understanding. That said, just because they are common doesn’t mean they are inevitable or should be ignored. If you are worried, encourage your friend to seek help.

Most college and university employees are considered “responsible employees” under Title IX and are obligated to report sexual assault. The exceptions are usually those with privilege under state law or a professional license requiring confidentiality. Examples may include therapists, professional and pastoral counselors, and advocates at qualifying crisis centers for sexual assault and intimate partner violence. If you are unsure whether or not you are considered a responsible employee, check your institution’s policy or ask your Title IX Coordinator. It is helpful to be aware of your reporting obligations before students discloses so that if they do, you can provide them with accurate information about what you need to report. Part of the school’s obligation is to ensure that employees know their obligations regarding reporting.

Under Title IX, responsible employees must report all relevant details about the assault that the student (or other person) has shared and that the school will need to determine what occurred and to resolve the situation. This includes the name of the victim, the name of the perpetrator (if known), other students involved as well as relevant facts including date, time and location. Campus-specific mandated reporting protocols. There are many ways that employees can become aware of a sexual assault, including through other students, personal essays and other writing assignments, and as a result of students asking for extensions or other accommodations. During a traumatic event, a person may respond in ways that do not seem to make sense. They may react differently from how you imagine you might react or differently from how they have reacted in other situations. After the traumatic event, they may behave in ways that seem strange or talk about the situation in ways that do not add up. However, when you learn about trauma, these responses and behaviors can start to make a lot more sense.

There are a number of ways people may react to sexual assault — not a single “right” or “wrong” response. Sometimes, it’s easy to understand why a person is unable to fight back or run away; they could have been too intoxicated, drugged, or otherwise physically unable to do so. Sometimes a person makes a decision that fighting back is more dangerous, and they want to survive the assault with as little physical injury as possible; so, they comply or submit to the assailant, but that’s not the same thing as consent
When met with a situation that threatens their survival or physical or emotional well-being, they often react with an automatic response designed to help. You might have heard of “fight or flight,” but it “fight, flight, freeze” more accurately captures the range of responses that a victim may have. In these situations, the brain triggers a release of hormones that affect our behavior. These can include the hormones that people often think of as stress hormones — the adrenaline that can make us feel “pumped up” and the cortisol that gives us energy. A surge of these hormones could result in fighting back or running away, if the victim is physically able to do so. There are also hormones that block pain, both physical and emotional, which can result in people feeling “out of it” or disconnected to the assault. Finally, there is also a hormone that causes positive feelings, which is another way to try to lessen the pain of the assault.
When someone experiences traumatic, parts of the brain that are responsible for survival are activated. The part of the brain that you use to make logical or rational decisions is often compromised. The way someone responds — flight, fight, or freeze — is a hardwired, automatic response meant to help us survive a critical threat. It isn’t a matter of making a right choice or wrong choice, because it isn’t even a conscious decision.

There is no wrong way to respond after the assault. Some people cry or yell, while others speak calmly. People can appear anxious, confused, withdrawn, or disconnected. They may have no affect at all or even laugh. There is no one correct way that all victims should respond.
As they try to talk about the assault, instead of going chronologically through the event, they may “jump around” in time or remember new details. This is also an outcome of the trauma suffered by the victim. Just as the hormone release associated with trauma can trigger an automatic behavioral response, it also interferes with memory. It does not mean the memories are false, just that the brain can’t store and retrieve them normally.

It can take a long time to heal from a traumatic event. The ways they react might not make sense. Some common responses to trauma include:
• feelings of guilt, shame, embarrassment, anger at self, self-blame
• acting like nothing happened – wanting to make everything go back to normal
• academic struggles and/or difficulty concentrating
• hypervigilance (extreme concern with their safety), jumpiness and/or a heightened startle response
• not wanting to be touched
• changes in eating, sleeping, or how they dress
• becoming socially withdrawn
• difficulty trusting people
• increased drinking or drug use
• engaging in self-injury or risky behaviors

These behaviors are common — so, if you see your friend doing any of the above, try to be compassionate and understanding. That said, just because they are common doesn’t mean they are inevitable or should be ignored. If you are worried, encourage your friend to seek help.
